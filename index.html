⏳ 1. Past Perfect (Simple & Continuous)

When to use: An action that happened before another action in the past.

Past Perfect Simple

Form: had + past participle (V3)

✅ She had finished her homework before dinner.
❌ She finished her homework before dinner had finished.

Past Perfect Continuous

Form: had been + verb-ing

✅ They had been waiting for an hour when the bus arrived.
❌ They waited for an hour when the bus had arrived.
Students often forget that Past Perfect needs another past event. Use timeline drawings. Common mistake: using Past Perfect without a second past reference.

🔧 2. Have Something Done (Causative)

Meaning: You arrange for someone else to do something for you.

Structure: have/has/had + object + past participle (V3)

✅ I had my hair cut yesterday. (Someone cut it for me)
✅ She is having her car repaired now.
❌ I had cut my hair. (That means you did it yourself!)
Emphasize the difference: "I painted my house" (I did it) vs "I had my house painted" (someone else did it). Perfect for translation tasks.

⚖️ 3. Determiners: both / either / neither

DeterminerMeaningExample
Bothtwo things togetherBoth students passed the test.
Eitherone or the other (2 options)You can choose either book.
Neithernot one and not the otherNeither answer is correct.
✅ Both of them are tired.
✅ Neither of us wants to fail.
❌ Either of the three options. (use "any" for more than two)
"Neither" is already negative, so avoid double negatives. "Both...and", "either...or", "neither...nor" are common patterns.

🔮 4. Modal Perfect (must/can’t/may + have + V3)

Used for deductions/speculations about the past.

must have + V3

Strong certainty something happened.

✅ She must have left early. (I'm sure she left)

can't have + V3

Strong certainty something didn't happen.

✅ He can't have forgotten the test! (impossible)

may/might have + V3

Possibility in the past.

✅ They may have missed the bus.
These are for past deduction, not obligation. Contrast: "You must go" (obligation now) vs "You must have gone" (past certainty).

🎨 5. Order of Adjectives

The natural English order: Opinion → Size → Age → Shape → Colour → Origin → Material → Purpose

✅ A beautiful small old round blue Italian ceramic plate.
❌ A blue Italian old small ceramic beautiful plate. (sounds weird)
Mnemonic: OSASCOMP (Opinion, Size, Age, Shape, Colour, Origin, Material, Purpose). Practice with noun phrases.

🚫 6. Non-continuous (Stative) Verbs

Verbs that describe states, not actions. Usually NOT used in continuous tenses.

Categories:
  • Mental states: know, believe, understand
  • Emotions: love, hate, prefer
  • Senses: see, hear, smell
  • Possession: have, own, belong
✅ I know the answer.
❌ I am knowing the answer.
✅ She wants some water.
❌ She is wanting water.
Some verbs can be both stative and dynamic (e.g., "think" - I think it's good vs I'm thinking about it). Highlight these.

🔗 7. Relative Clauses (Noun / Adjective / Adverb clauses)

Adjective clause describes a noun. Noun clause acts as subject/object. Often tested together.

Adjective (Relative) Clauses

✅ The student who studies hard will pass.
✅ The book that you gave me is great.

Noun Clauses

✅ What she said surprised me. (subject)
✅ I don't know where he went. (object)
Common test trap: mixing "what" and "that". "I know that you are tired" vs "I know what you mean". Practice recognition.

✍️ Section 1 Strategy: Correct the Mistake

Common mistakes aligned with topics:

❌ I am knowing her since 2019. → ✅ I have known her since 2019. (stative verb)
❌ She had went to school. → ✅ She had gone to school. (past perfect)
❌ I had my car repair. → ✅ I had my car repaired. (causative)
❌ Both student didn't come. → ✅ Neither student came. (determiner)
❌ He must left early. → ✅ He must have left early. (modal perfect)
